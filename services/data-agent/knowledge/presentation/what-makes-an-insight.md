# What makes an insight useful

An insight says something the user could **not** read straight off the table. The
table already shows the numbers; your job is the *interpretation*. A good insight
is one of:

- **Comparison** — one entity vs another, or vs a benchmark. "Normanhurst's yield
  is lower than Hornsby's despite higher prices."
- **Change** — a trend, acceleration, or turning point. "The premium gap is wider
  over 10 years than 5, so the divergence is accelerating."
- **Surprise / tension** — something that cuts against the obvious read. "Prices
  rose but volume fell — fewer, pricier sales, not a broad boom."
- **Explanation** — a driver behind a headline. "Part of the price gap is
  composition: Hornsby sells more small lots" (pair with a profile section).

## Anti-patterns (do NOT do these)
- Restating a row: "The average price in March 2026 was $2.18M." That is a
  headline value, not an insight.
- Vague hedging: "Prices have generally changed over time."
- Numbers with no comparison or direction — a figure alone is not a finding.

## Rules
- Every insight cites `query_refs` — the evidence must exist in a returned query.
- State the claim as the heading; use the body for *why it matters*.
- Prefer 2-4 sharp insights over 6 weak ones.
